12  Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

NoteKey idea

A policy can work on average and still work very differently across units. Heterogeneous treatment effect analysis asks who benefits, who does not, and can provide insights into why the same policy may produce different effects for different units.

Most impact evaluation methods are introduced by focusing on average effects. We often ask whether a policy works on average, and summarize its impact using parameters such as the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT), or the local average treatment effect (LATE).

This is a natural starting point. Because of the fundamental problem of causal inference, we cannot observe both potential outcomes for the same unit (Holland 1986; Rubin 1974). We therefore usually move from individual-level causal effects to average causal effects.

However, average effects may hide important variation. A policy may be highly effective for some units, ineffective for others, and even harmful for another group. In many policy settings, knowing the average effects of a policy change is not enough. We also want to know for whom it works, where it works, and under which conditions it works best.

NoteKey idea

Heterogeneous treatment effect (HTE) analysis asks whether the causal effect of a treatment varies across units, groups, or contexts.

12.1 From Average Effects to HTEs

Let \(Y_i(1)\) and \(Y_i(0)\) denote the potential outcomes of unit \(i\) under treatment and no treatment. The individual treatment effect is

\[ \tau_i = Y_i(1)-Y_i(0). \]

If treatment effects were the same for all units, then

\[ \tau_i = \tau \]

for every unit \(i\). In that special case, ATE, ATT, and LATE would all coincide.

In practice, this assumption is often unrealistic. Treatment effects may vary by age, gender, education, income, firm size, local labor-market conditions, political context, baseline risk, or many other pre-treatment characteristics.

For example, a job-search assistance program may be more effective for workers with intermediate skills than for workers who are either very highly skilled or very weakly attached to the labor market. A subsidy to firms may have larger effects among small liquidity-constrained firms than among large firms. A drug may help some patients but harm others.

The object of interest is often the conditional average treatment effect, or CATE:

\[ CATE(x) = E[Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)\mid X_i=x]. \]

The CATE measures the average treatment effect for units with characteristics \(X_i=x\).

NoteCATE

The CATE answers the question:
What is the average treatment effect for units with a given set of pre-treatment characteristics?

Sometimes researchers focus on a single characteristic, such as gender, age group, or firm size. In other cases, they are interested in richer combinations of covariates. The main challenge is that heterogeneity may be multidimensional and nonlinear.

12.2 Why Heterogeneity Matters

HTEs matter for several reasons.

  1. They improve interpretation. If the average effect is close to zero, this does not necessarily mean that the policy had no effect. It may mean that positive effects for some units were offset by negative effects for others.

  2. They matter for targeting. Policymakers often need to decide who should receive a program in the future. If the policy is costly, it may be useful to target it toward the units that benefit most.

  3. Heterogeneity can reveal causal mechanisms. If effects are larger for some groups than for others, this may help us understand why the policy works.

  4. Heterogeneity is important for equity. A policy that improves average outcomes may still increase inequality if its benefits are concentrated among already advantaged groups.

TipPolicy relevance

A policy evaluation should not only ask whether a program works on average.
It should also ask whether the program works better or worse for different types of units.

12.3 The Most Common Approach for Estimating HTEs

Although there exist several ways to study HTEs, post-conditioning on relevant subgroups is by far the most adopted. This generally consists of estimating the ATT and then examine whether the effect differs across subgroups. For example, researchers may estimate separate effects by gender, age, education, income, region, or firm size. This is a top-down approach: the researcher chooses the relevant subgroups based on theory, institutional knowledge, or prior evidence. For example, in a study of the electoral effects of refugee reception centers, one may ask whether the effect differs across municipalities with different income levels, political histories, or demographic structures.

The advantage of this approach is that it is easy to understand and communicate. Its main limitation is that the estimation process can easily become arbitrary and vulnerable to cherry-picking: researchers may try many subgroup definitions and report only those that produce statistically significant differences. This creates risks of multiple testing, selective reporting, and p-hacking (Brand et al. 2021).

WarningThe risk of subgroup fishing

Subgroup analysis is most credible when the relevant groups are motivated before looking at the results.
If many subgroups are explored and only significant findings are reported, inference becomes unreliable.

This is also a multiple testing problem: the more subgroup comparisons we try, the more likely it is that some statistically significant differences will appear simply by chance.

A second limitation is that one-variable-at-a-time subgroup analysis may miss complex patterns. Treatment effects may vary not simply by income or education separately, but by combinations of characteristics.

NoteA bottom-up approach to heterogeneity

An alternative approach is to estimate treatment effects at a very disaggregated level and then study which characteristics are associated with larger or smaller effects. This is a more bottom-up strategy: instead of starting from pre-defined subgroups, the researcher first explores how effects vary across units and then tries to explain this variation using observed covariates.

This approach can be useful when rich data are available and when the relevant dimensions of heterogeneity are not fully known in advance. However, these estimates should be interpreted with caution. Individual treatment effects are often noisy and should not be considered precisely estimated individual causal effects. A practical consequence is that researchers may end up overinterpreting random variation as meaningful heterogeneity, identifying apparent “winners” and “losers” of the treatment even when these differences are largely driven by estimation noise.

12.4 Data-driven estimation of HTEs

Another approach estimates treatment effects and their heterogeneity at the same time. This is the logic behind methods such as causal trees and causal forests.

These methods are designed to discover subgroups with different treatment effects in a data-driven way. They are especially useful when the researcher has many potential moderators and does not know in advance which combinations of covariates define the most responsive groups.

Compared with traditional subgroup analysis, these methods can reduce researcher discretion and help detect nonlinear interactions. However, they also require careful implementation and strong identifying assumptions, especially in observational studies.

12.4.1 Decision Trees and Random Forests

Causal trees and causal forests build on machine learning methods originally designed for prediction.

A decision tree partitions the covariate space into regions. In a regression tree, the goal is to predict an outcome \(Y_i\). The algorithm searches for splits of the covariates that create groups with similar outcome values.

For example, a regression tree predicting wine quality may first split wines by alcohol content, then split one branch by acidity, and another by sulphates. Each final leaf contains observations with similar predicted outcomes.

Decision trees are attractive because they are easy to interpret. However, they can be unstable: small changes in the data may generate a different tree.

A random forest addresses this instability by estimating many trees and averaging their predictions. Each tree is built on a different sample and uses a random subset of covariates when choosing splits. This usually improves predictive accuracy, but the resulting model is less transparent than a single tree.

12.4.2 Why Standard Machine Learning Is Not Enough

Standard decision trees and random forests are designed to predict outcomes. But causal inference has a different goal: estimating treatment effects.

A method that predicts \(Y_i\) very well does not necessarily estimate causal effects well. Predicting outcomes and estimating unbiased treatment effects are different problems.

For this reason, standard machine learning tools should not be used directly to estimate causal effects without modification. They must be adapted to the causal problem.

NotePrediction is not causation

A model can predict outcomes accurately and still fail to estimate causal effects.
Causal trees and causal forests modify tree-based methods so that they target treatment effect heterogeneity rather than outcome prediction.

12.4.3 Causal Trees

Causal trees, introduced by Athey and Imbens, adapt decision trees to estimate heterogeneous treatment effects (Athey and Imbens 2016).

Instead of splitting the data to improve prediction of \(Y_i\), causal trees split the data to find groups with different treatment effects. The goal is to create leaves where treatment effects are relatively homogeneous within each leaf but differ across leaves.

Each terminal leaf of the tree contains an estimate of the treatment effect for units in that subgroup.

For example, a causal tree may find that a training programme has a large effect for young workers with low previous earnings, a smaller effect for older workers, and no effect for workers with high baseline employment probabilities.

The key challenge is that the true individual treatment effect is never observed. Unlike standard prediction problems, we do not have a ground-truth outcome equal to \(\tau_i\) for each unit.

Causal trees address this by using criteria that reward splits producing meaningful differences in estimated treatment effects while penalizing imprecise estimates.

12.4.4 Honest Estimation

A central idea in causal trees is honesty. The data are split into two parts:

  • one part is used to build the tree;
  • the other part is used to estimate treatment effects within the leaves.

This helps reduce overfitting. If the same data are used both to choose the subgroups and to estimate the effects, the estimated heterogeneity may be exaggerated. Honest estimation separates discovery from estimation.

TipHonest trees

Honest causal trees use one part of the data to decide the tree structure and another part to estimate treatment effects within the leaves.

The price is that each task uses less data. This can reduce precision, especially in small samples.

12.4.5 Causal Trees in Observational Studies

In randomized experiments, treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes by design. In observational studies, this is not true. Causal trees must therefore rely on identifying assumptions, typically some version of unconfoundedness.

The idea is that, after controlling for observed covariates, treatment assignment is as good as random. If this assumption is credible, causal trees can be combined with methods such as propensity score adjustment or inverse probability weighting.

The important point is that causal trees do not automatically solve confounding. They help discover heterogeneity conditional on the assumptions that make causal identification possible.

12.4.6 Causal Forests

A single causal tree is interpretable but may be unstable. A causal forest extends the logic of random forests to treatment effect estimation.

Instead of growing one causal tree, a causal forest grows many causal trees and combines their information to estimate conditional treatment effects (Wager and Athey 2018).

Causal forests are less visually transparent than a single tree, but they are usually more stable and flexible. They can handle many covariates and complex interactions, and they can provide useful summaries of heterogeneity.

Common outputs include:

  • estimated CATEs for individual units or subgroups;
  • tests for the presence of treatment effect heterogeneity;
  • variable importance measures;
  • subgroup summaries based on predicted treatment effects.

For example, a causal forest may show that the effect of job displacement on earnings differs substantially across workers, establishments, and local labor markets (Athey et al. 2024).

12.4.7 Interpreting Causal Forests

Causal forests can be powerful, but they require careful interpretation.

First, the estimates are not individual treatment effects observed in the data. They are predictions of conditional treatment effects based on patterns in the data.

Second, the results depend on the identifying assumptions. If treatment assignment is confounded by unobserved variables, causal forests may discover heterogeneity in selection bias rather than heterogeneity in causal effects.

Third, variable importance measures should not be interpreted mechanically. A variable may appear important because it helps define treatment effect heterogeneity, but the meaning of that heterogeneity still requires substantive interpretation.

WarningBe careful with causal forests

Causal forests can be useful tools for exploring HTEs, but their unit-level predictions should not be interpreted as precisely estimated individual causal effects. These methods build on recursive partitioning, and the resulting estimates can be locally noisy, especially in sparse regions of the covariate space or when some leaves contain few observations.

Recent theoretical results show that even causal trees based on standard splitting rules may perform poorly at the local level, even when average accuracy looks acceptable (Cattaneo et al. 2026). This means that a model may appear to work well overall while still producing unreliable effect estimates for specific units or small subgroups.

For this reason, causal forests are best used as exploratory tools for detecting broad patterns of heterogeneity, not as automatic machines for identifying precisely which units benefit more or less from a policy.

12.5 Practical Guidance on the estimation of HTEs

A credible analysis of heterogeneous treatment effects should report:

  • why heterogeneity is substantively important;
  • whether subgroup analyses were pre-specified or exploratory;
  • which covariates are used to define heterogeneity;
  • how multiple testing is handled;
  • whether the analysis relies on unconfoundedness, randomization, IV, RDD, or another design;
  • whether the estimated heterogeneity is statistically meaningful;
  • how large the subgroups are;
  • whether results are robust across alternative specifications or methods.

When using causal trees or causal forests, it is also useful to report how the data were split, how tuning parameters were chosen, and which covariates are most important in explaining heterogeneity.

WarningHeterogeneity needs design too

Flexible methods can find patterns, but they do not create causal identification by themselves.
The credibility of HTE analysis still depends on the underlying research design.

12.6 Summary

Average treatment effects are useful, but they may hide important variation. Heterogeneous treatment effect analysis asks whether a policy works differently for different units.

Traditional subgroup analysis is simple and transparent, but it can be vulnerable to multiple testing and selective reporting. More flexible approaches, such as causal trees and causal forests, can discover complex heterogeneity across many covariates, but they require careful implementation and interpretation.

The main message is that heterogeneity analysis should not be treated as a purely technical exercise. It should be connected to theory, policy relevance, and the credibility of the underlying causal design.
