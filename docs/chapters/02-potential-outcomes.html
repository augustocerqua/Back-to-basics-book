2  The Potential Outcomes Framework

The potential outcomes framework provides the basic language of causal inference. Its central idea is simple: to define a causal effect, we must compare what would happen to the same unit under different treatment states. These alternative outcomes are called potential outcomes.

The origins of this framework go back to the work of Jerzy Neyman on randomized agricultural experiments first published in 1927 (Neyman 1990). Neyman introduced the idea that each experimental unit has potential outcomes under different treatments, even though only one of them is observed. Later, Donald Rubin extended this logic beyond randomized experiments, turning it into a general framework for causal inference in both experimental and observational studies (Rubin 1974, 1978). For this reason, the approach is often called the Neyman–Rubin potential outcomes framework or the Rubin causal model.

Suppose we are interested in the effect of a binary treatment \(D_i\) on an outcome \(Y_i\) for unit \(i\), where:

\[ D_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if unit } i \text{ receives the treatment},\\ 0 & \text{if unit } i \text{ does not receive the treatment}. \end{cases} \]

For each unit, we define two potential outcomes:

\[ Y_i(1) \quad \text{and} \quad Y_i(0). \]

Here, \(Y_i(1)\) is the outcome that unit \(i\) would experience if treated, while \(Y_i(0)\) is the outcome that the same unit would experience if untreated. The individual treatment effect (ITE) is therefore

\[ \Delta_i = Y_i(1)-Y_i(0). \]

This definition captures the essence of causal inference: a causal effect is the difference between two potential states of the same unit. For example, if the treatment is receiving a public subsidy aimed at reducing workplace accidents, the causal effect for a given firm is the difference between the number of workplace accidents with the subsidy at time \(t_2\) and the number of workplace accidents that the same firm would have experienced without the subsidy at time \(t_2\).

The problem is that we never observe both potential outcomes for the same unit. If the unit is treated, we observe \(Y_i(1)\) but not \(Y_i(0)\). If the unit is untreated, we observe \(Y_i(0)\) but not \(Y_i(1)\). The observed outcome can be written as

\[ Y_i^{obs} = D_iY_i(1) + (1-D_i)Y_i(0). \]

This means that \(Y_i^{obs}=Y_i(1)\) for treated units, for which \(D_i=1\), and \(Y_i^{obs}=Y_i(0)\) for untreated units, for which \(D_i=0\). In other words, we observe only the potential outcome corresponding to the treatment status actually received.

The missing potential outcome is the counterfactual. This is why causal inference is fundamentally a missing-data problem: one potential outcome is observed, while the other must be inferred. Holland famously called this the fundamental problem of causal inference (Holland 1986).

ImportantThe fundamental problem of causal inference

For each unit, we observe only one potential outcome.
The other potential outcome is missing: it is counterfactual and cannot be directly observed. This missingness is not a data collection accident, but a structural feature of causal inference, because each unit can be observed under either the treated or untreated state.

For this reason, individual treatment effects are impossible to observe directly. As a second-best solution, causal inference usually focuses on average treatment effects: instead of asking how the treatment affected each individual unit, we ask how it affected a group of units on average (discussed in Chapter 3).

The reason is that, although we cannot observe both potential outcomes for the same unit, we can observe different units under different treatment statuses. If treated and untreated units are sufficiently similar in the relevant pre-treatment characteristics, the average outcome of the untreated units may provide a credible approximation of the missing counterfactual outcome for the treated units: what they would have experienced, on average, in the absence of treatment. In this sense, causal inference replaces the impossible comparison of the same unit under two alternative states of the world with a comparison between groups of units that are credible substitutes for one another, except for the treatment.

2.1 Potential Outcomes and Randomized Controlled Trials

In randomized experiments, treatment assignment is controlled by the researcher. In a successful RCT, treatment status is independent of the units’ potential outcomes. In simple terms, this means that the treatment is not assigned to units because they are expected to do better or worse. Randomization makes treatment assignment unrelated to the outcomes units would have experienced with or without the treatment. Formally, random assignment implies

\[ (Y_i(1),Y_i(0)) \perp D_i. \]

This means that treated and control groups are comparable on average, not necessarily unit by unit. The logic of randomization is therefore statistical: it works by making the two groups similar as groups, not by matching each treated unit with an identical untreated unit. By the law of large numbers, as the number of units increases, random assignment tends to balance both observed and unobserved characteristics across treatment and control groups.

This also clarifies why randomizing only two units, one treated and one untreated, is not enough to make the RCT logic convincing. Even if treatment is randomly assigned, the two units may still differ substantially for reasons unrelated to the treatment. With only two units, there is no averaging that washes out individual idiosyncrasies or pre-existing differences.

With larger samples, instead, the average outcome of the control group provides an estimate of what the treated group would have experienced without treatment. Similarly, the average outcome of the treated group provides information about outcomes under treatment. In formal terms,

\[ E[Y_i(0)\mid D_i=1] = E[Y_i(0)\mid D_i=0]. \]

Randomization does not reveal the missing counterfactual for each individual unit. It solves the counterfactual problem on average, by creating a credible comparison group that reveals the missing average counterfactual.

This is why, under randomization, average treatment effects can be credibly estimated even when individual treatment effects cannot be observed. RCTs are therefore often considered the benchmark for causal inference: by design, they solve the counterfactual problem for average treatment effects (Neyman 1990; Rubin 1978).

2.2 Observational Studies

In many social science and policy settings, random assignment is not feasible. Treatment assignment is often shaped by choices, rules, or exposure conditions that are not controlled by the researcher. Individuals, firms, schools, or neighborhoods may select into treatment; policymakers may target specific units; and some units may be more likely to experience certain shocks because of their characteristics or location. Natural disasters provide a simple example: earthquakes are more likely to affect areas near fault lines, while hurricanes are more likely to hit coastal areas exposed to tropical storm paths.

As a result, treated and untreated units may differ even before the treatment occurs. Some of these differences may be observable, while others may be unobservable. If pre-existing differences also affect the outcome of interest, a simple post-treatment comparison may be misleading: it may capture not only the effect of the treatment, but also differences that were already present before treatment assignment.

This is why simple comparisons may suffer from selection bias.

It is also why many empirical researchers look for natural experiments. In a natural experiment, an institutional rule, a policy change, a threshold, timing, or an external shock generates variation in treatment that is plausibly unrelated to the units’ potential outcomes. This does not make the study automatically equivalent to an RCT, but it can bring observational data closer to the logic of random assignment. The hope is to exploit these quasi-random sources of variation to construct a more credible counterfactual and estimate causal parameters under more plausible assumptions.

Rubin’s contribution was to show that the same potential outcomes logic can be applied to observational studies (Rubin 1974). The goal is to reconstruct, as closely as possible, the comparison that a randomized experiment would have produced. Different causal methods do this in different ways. Matching, weighting, DiD, RDD, IV with a binary instrument, and SCM all use information from untreated units to recover credible counterfactual outcomes, but they do so under different assumptions. Still, they share some common identifying assumptions, which are discussed in the next section.

2.3 Key Assumptions

The potential outcomes framework is not, by itself, an estimator. It provides a formal language for defining causal effects and clarifies what needs to be observed, or assumed, in order to estimate them. In its basic version, several assumptions are especially important and underlie all control-based evaluation strategies.

1) Manipulable treatment. The treatment must correspond to a clear intervention that could, at least in principle, be assigned or manipulated. This idea is often summarized by the motto: no causation without manipulation.

For example, attending a training program, receiving a subsidy, or gaining access to a nursery school can be interpreted as treatments. By contrast, characteristics such as nationality at birth or age are not treatments in the same direct sense, because they cannot be assigned by an intervention.

NoteGoing deeper

A related issue arises when a policy does not vary across units or over time. If all units are always observed under the same policy regime, there is no variation in treatment status that can be exploited for identification. A similar problem arises in panel-data designs when some units are always treated and others are never treated: although treatment varies across units, there is no within-unit change.

Consider Acemoglu et al.’s study of the effect of democracy on economic growth (Acemoglu et al. 2019). Their analysis exploits transitions into and out of democracy to examine how countries’ economic trajectories change following a change in political regime. Imagine instead that every country had remained either a democracy or a dictatorship throughout the entire observation period. There would be no regime transition to exploit. Researchers could still compare always-democratic countries with always-autocratic countries, but these groups might differ in history, institutions, geography, and many other determinants of economic growth. Interpreting this cross-sectional difference causally would therefore require much stronger assumptions.

Potential outcomes under alternative regimes can still be defined. The problem is empirical: without units switching status, untreated comparison units, or another credible source of variation, the observed data provide limited information about what would have happened under a different regime. The causal effect is therefore not identified without additional assumptions or an alternative research design.

2) Consistency. Consistency links the potential outcomes to the outcome we actually observe in the data. Once we know whether unit \(i\) was treated or untreated, consistency tells us which potential outcome becomes observed.

If unit \(i\) receives the treatment, we observe its treated potential outcome:

\[ Y_i^{obs} = Y_i(1) \quad \text{if } D_i=1. \]

If unit \(i\) does not receive the treatment, we observe its untreated potential outcome:

\[ Y_i^{obs} = Y_i(0) \quad \text{if } D_i=0. \]

In simple terms, consistency says that the observed outcome is the potential outcome corresponding to the treatment status actually received. It is the bridge between the theoretical notation, \(Y_i(1)\) and \(Y_i(0)\), and the outcome observed in the dataset.

3) Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption. A central assumption of the basic potential outcomes framework is the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA) (Rubin 1980). This assumption allows us to write only two potential outcomes for each unit: \(Y_i(1)\) and \(Y_i(0)\).

SUTVA has two main components.

  1. There should be no hidden versions of the treatment. The treatment must be sufficiently homogeneous across treated units. If some units receive a light version of the program and others receive a much more intensive version, then the notation \(Y_i(1)\) is too vague. There is not one treatment, but several different treatments.

  2. There should be no interference between units. The potential outcome of one unit should depend only on that unit’s own treatment status, not on the treatment status of other units. This idea is often traced back to Cox’s discussion of interference between experimental units (Cox 1958).

Formally, in the simple version of the framework, we write

\[ Y_i = Y_i(D_i). \]

This notation assumes a binary treatment: unit \(i\)’s outcome depends only on its own treatment status \(D_i\), which can take only two values, 0 and 1. If instead unit \(i\)’s outcome also depends on whether other units are treated, the notation would need to be expanded.

WarningWhen SUTVA may fail

SUTVA may fail when the treatment has different versions or intensities, or when it generates spillovers, peer effects, displacement effects, or general equilibrium effects.

SUTVA can fail in two main ways:

  1. Different versions or intensities of the treatment. Suppose that a school introduces a tutoring program for students with low test scores. Some students receive one-to-one tutoring, while others receive group tutoring. If we treat all participants as if they had received the same intervention, we are mixing different versions of the treatment. In this case, the estimated effect would not refer to a clearly defined program. A similar problem arises when the treatment varies in intensity. For example, students may receive different amounts of tutoring depending on their needs: some may attend only one hour per week, others three, five, seven, or even more. If we collapse all these different exposure levels into a single binary indicator of participation, the notation \(Y_i(1)\) becomes too vague. The problem is not that continuous treatments are incompatible with the potential outcomes framework. Rather, the treatment must be defined accordingly. For instance, we may write potential outcomes as \(Y_i(d)\), where \(d\) denotes treatment intensity. What violates the first component of SUTVA is treating different doses or versions as if they were the same treatment. (See Chapter 14 for a deeper discussion.)

  2. The treatment received by one unit may affect the outcomes of other units. Suppose that students who receive tutoring share materials, explain exercises, or study together with classmates who were not selected for the program. In this case, untreated students are no longer unaffected by the intervention: their outcomes may improve because of their interaction with treated students. This violates the no-interference component of SUTVA. The comparison between tutored and non-tutored students would therefore be contaminated: the control group is not simply untreated, but indirectly exposed to the treatment. Whether control-based methods can still be used in similar scenarios depends on the extent and structure of the spillover effects. If spillovers affect only a clearly identifiable subset of untreated units, researchers may exclude those units from the control group or explicitly treat them as indirectly exposed. In this case, unaffected untreated units may still provide a credible counterfactual. By contrast, if spillovers reach all untreated units, there may be no ‘clean’ control group left, and standard control-based methods cannot recover the causal parameters of interest without additional strong assumptions. Importantly, spillover effects may also affect treated units. For example, tutored students may benefit not only from the tutoring program itself, but also from interactions with other tutored students. In this case, the causal effect of the treatment on the treated no longer captures only the direct effect of receiving the treatment. It may also include indirect effects generated by the treatment status of other units. It is therefore necessary to clarify exactly which causal effect is being estimated. (See Chapter 13 for a deeper discussion.)

2.4 Assignment Mechanism

To estimate causal effects, we also need information about how treatment is assigned.

In randomized experiments, assignment is random:

\[ (Y_i(1),Y_i(0)) \perp D_i. \]

This means that treatment assignment is unrelated to what would have happened to units under either treatment condition. In other words, units expected to do better or worse are not systematically more likely to receive the treatment. Randomization makes treatment status unrelated to the potential outcomes units would have experienced with or without the treatment. As a result, the treated and control groups are comparable on average, and the control group can be used to estimate the missing counterfactual outcome for the treated group.

In observational studies, this is usually not true. For instance, let us consider the case of matching methods (see Chapter 5 for a deeper discussion.). The key identifying assumption is the conditional independence assumption (CIA):

\[ (Y_i(1),Y_i(0)) \perp D_i \mid X_i. \]

This means that, after conditioning on pre-treatment or time-invariant observed covariates \(X_i\), treatment assignment is as good as random. In other words, among units with the same, or sufficiently similar, values of \(X_i\), treated and untreated units are comparable, and differences in their post-treatment outcomes can be interpreted as estimates of causal effects.

It is important to recognize that this assumption should generally be considered quite strong. It requires that all variables affecting both treatment assignment and potential outcomes are observed and correctly accounted for.

In other words, after conditioning on \(X_i\), there should be no remaining systematic differences between treated and untreated units that are related to the outcome. This is demanding because, in observational studies, treatment assignment often depends on factors that are difficult to measure. For example, workers may enter a training program because they are more motivated, firms may apply for subsidies because they have better managers, and neighborhoods may receive a policy because policymakers expect them to respond more strongly.

If these factors are unobserved and also affect the outcome, then treated and untreated units are still not comparable after conditioning on \(X_i\). In that case, CIA fails, and the estimated treatment effect may still reflect selection bias rather than the causal effect of the treatment.

A further difficulty is that, in observational studies, the assignment mechanism is often only partially understood. We usually do not know exactly why some units receive the treatment and others do not, or why they receive it at a particular time, place, or intensity. This makes it harder to assess whether the identifying assumptions behind a given evaluation strategy are plausible. For this reason, the assumptions used in observational studies should not be treated as mechanical conditions that can be satisfied by choosing a sophisticated estimator or adding many controls. They are substantive claims about how treatment assignment took place and about why the resulting comparison can be interpreted as a credible counterfactual.

2.5 Summary

NoteKey idea

Causal inference is not just about comparing outcomes.
It is about comparing observed outcomes with credible counterfactual outcomes.

The potential outcomes framework defines causal effects by comparing potential outcomes under treatment and no treatment. Its main contribution is to make the counterfactual problem explicit.

For each unit, the individual treatment effect is

\[ \Delta_i = Y_i(1)-Y_i(0), \]

but only one of these two outcomes is observed. This makes individual causal effects generally impossible to measure directly.

Randomized experiments solve this problem on average by making treatment assignment independent of potential outcomes. Randomization does not make every treated unit identical to every untreated unit, but it makes the two groups comparable in expectation. Observational studies try to approximate this logic using assumptions and research designs that make treated and untreated units comparable.

The simple binary-treatment framework relies on a few key assumptions: the treatment must be manipulable, it must be well defined, and one unit’s outcome must not depend on the treatment status of other units. When these conditions fail, or when the treatment is not binary, the simple notation \(Y_i(1)\) and \(Y_i(0)\) may no longer be sufficient, and the causal question must be reformulated more carefully.
